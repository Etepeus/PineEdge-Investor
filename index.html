02

The Market Problem

Fragmented Structure. Limited Liquidity. No Infrastructure Layer.

Physical commodity markets — particularly outside major exchange-traded products — remain structurally under-digitised and relationship-dependent. Supply and demand exist, what is missing is structured digital infrastructure between them.

Fragmentation: Thousands of producers and buyers transact in isolated networks with limited price visibility.
Opacity: Pricing is negotiated privately, with minimal standardisation or public benchmarks.
Limited Liquidity: Without structured listing and discovery infrastructure, assets remain illiquid.
No Digital Standardisation: Commodities are traded physically, but rarely defined digitally in a repeatable, programmable way.

The inefficiency in physical commodity markets is not due to lack of demand — it is due to the absence of structured digital infrastructure.

🧩 Fragmentation
Physical commodity supply is highly distributed. Producers operate across regions, sectors, and scale tiers — from large industrial operators to smaller specialised suppliers. Buyers similarly range from corporates to trading houses to niche industrial processors. Transactions are typically conducted within existing relationship networks.

As a result:
  • Discovery is manual
  • Buyer reach is limited
  • Supply visibility is constrained
  • Market depth is unclear

Fragmentation does not eliminate demand — it suppresses efficient matching.
🌫️ Opacity

Pricing in many physical commodity sectors remains bilateral and confidential.Unlike exchange-traded commodities.

Many markets lack:

  • Centralised listings
  • Transparent bid/offer environments
  • Repeatable benchmarking
  • Public reference pricing

This opacity reduces price confidence, discourages new entrants, and increases dependency on intermediaries. Capital prefers transparency. Opaque markets struggle to attract it.

💧 Limited Liquidity

Liquidity is not solely a function of supply and demand. It is a function of structured infrastructure.

Without:

  • Standardised listings
  • Discoverable inventory
  • Efficient transaction mechanics
  • Reliable documentation

Assets remain economically illiquid. Capital cannot deploy at scale into markets that cannot demonstrate velocity, comparability, and structured trade history. The constraint is structural — not economic.

⚙️ No Digital Standardisation

Physical commodities are traded through contracts — but rarely defined as structured digital units.

  • Lot definitions vary.
  • Specifications differ.
  • Documentation is inconsistent.
  • Provenance is fragmented.

Without digital standardisation:
  • Data cannot compound
  • Trade history cannot scale
  • Assets cannot become programmable
  • Financialisation is constrained

Modern capital markets operate on structured digital instruments. Most of physical commodity trade does not.
